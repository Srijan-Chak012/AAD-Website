History

The first documented application of cryptography (in some form) was discovered in an inscription carved circa 1900 BC in the main chamber of the tomb of the Egyptian nobleman Khnumhotep II. In places, the scribe substituted strange hieroglyphic characters with more common ones. The goal was not to conceal the message, but rather to adapt its shape in such a manner that it appeared respectable. Most significant early civilizations have evidence of some usage of cryptography. Kautalya's classic text on statecraft, "Arthshashtra," discusses the Indian spy service and includes assigning spies instructions in "secret writing."

Julius Caesar was known to utilise encryption to send secret communications to his army generals stationed on the battlefield. This substitution cypher, known as the Caesar cypher, is maybe the most discussed historic encryption in academic literature. To construct the cypher text, each character in the plain text is replaced by another character in a substitution cypher. Caesar's version was a three-cipher shift. Each character was relocated by three positions, thus 'A' was replaced by 'D,' 'B' by 'E,' and so on.

During the 16th century, Vigenere created a cypher that was allegedly the first to employ an encryption key. In one of his cyphers, the encryption key was repeated numerous times over the whole message, and the cypher text was generated by multiplying the message character by modulo 26.

Vigenere's cypher, like the Caesar cypher, is readily cracked; nevertheless, Vigenere's cypher introduced the concept of encryption keys into the picture, though it was poorly handled. When compared to Caesar cypher, the message's secret is determined by the secrecy of the encryption key rather than the system's secrecy.

When everything went electric at the beginning of the 20th century, Hebern created the Hebern rotor machine, an electro-mechanical device. It makes use of a single rotor, with the secret key placed in a revolving disc. The key encoded a substitution table, and each key press on the keyboard resulted in encrypted text output. This also rotated the disc by one notch, resulting in a new table being utilised for the following plain text character. This was broken one more by employing letter frequencies.

The Enigma

The Enigma machine is an encryption device that was invented and used in the early to mid-twentieth century to secure commercial, diplomatic, and military communication. During World War II, Nazi Germany used it extensively in all departments of the German military. The Germans mistakenly felt that using the Enigma system enabled them to communicate securely and so gain a significant edge in World War II. The Enigma machine was thought to be so safe that it was used to encrypt even the most top-secret transmissions.

Marian Rejewski, a Polish mathematician and cryptanalyst of the Polish Cipher Bureau, exploited the idea of permutations and faults in German military message encipherment methods to crack the message keys of the plugboard Enigma machine around December 1932. Starting in January 1933, the Polish Cipher Bureau devised procedures to bypass the plugboard and locate all components of the daily key, allowing the Cipher Bureau to decode German Enigma transmissions.

Alan Turing created the British bombe shortly after arriving at Bletchley Park in September 1939. The bombe assisted in determining the wheel order, beginning locations of the rotor cores, and the stecker partner of a given letter. This was accomplished by assessing all 17,576 potential scrambler positions given a set of wheel commands based on a crib and ciphertext comparison, in order to exclude options that violated the Enigma's known features.

The detachable drums on the front of the bombe were wired in the same way as the connections formed by Enigma's several rotors. In contrast to them, the input and output connections for the left and right sides were independent, resulting in 104 contacts between each drum and the remainder of the machine. This enables a series of scramblers to be linked using 26-way wires. Metal brushes were used to make electrical connections between the wiring of the revolving drums and the rear plugboard. When the bombe identified a scrambler position with no inconsistencies, it would come to a halt, and the operator would take note of it before resuming it.
